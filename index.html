23 February 2026 Weeks 1–2

First Impressions and Building the Foundation

What I Worked On

The first two weeks at the Swedish Macedonian Chamber of Commerce have been a whirlwind. My primary task was to build the organization’s website from scratch. I was given a brief and some brand guidelines, and from there it was largely up to me to plan the structure, design the layout, and implement everything. This required strong front-end skills like HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, as well as an understanding of user experience principles from my studies. One of my biggest realizations was how different it is to build something for a real organization compared to a school project. Every decision carries more weight when real users are going to interact with the result.

Deadlines & Planning

The initial deadline for the website’s first version was met on time. I planned my work by breaking the site into sections and tackling them one by one. I have now been working on the website for over a month and a half, and I am already contributing to the team’s social media output as well. For the coming weeks, the plan is to continue refining the website based on feedback and begin exploring the backend requirements.

What I Learned

Beyond the technical skills I practiced, I learned a great deal about working within an organization that operates across two countries. Communication happens in different time zones and across cultural boundaries, which taught me the importance of being clear and proactive in my messages. I also gained a deeper appreciation for how much effort goes into managing an organization from the inside. There are so many moving parts that aren’t visible from the outside.

The Environment

The team is larger than I expected, over 20 people, and everyone has been welcoming. I’ve already met several professionals from various business backgrounds, which has been eye-opening. My colleagues come with different values and working styles, and I’m learning to adapt my communication accordingly. The work environment is quite pleasant and motivating, which makes it easy to stay engaged. Being treated as “the IT guy” from day one was a bit surprising, but it also gave me an immediate sense of responsibility and ownership over the digital side of things.

Strengths & Growth

I feel confident in my front-end development skills, which allowed me to deliver the website on schedule. Where I see room for growth is in project estimation. I underestimated how long certain design decisions would take when they need stakeholder approval. On the soft-skills side, I’m getting better at professional communication, but I want to improve my ability to present technical ideas to non-technical colleagues.

Looking Ahead

Overall, I’m genuinely enjoying this internship. The work is engaging, the people are interesting, and I can already feel that this experience is opening doors. I see real potential in creating a digital solution here that could become something valuable, not just for the organization, but as a learning experience for my future career. I’m excited for what the coming weeks will bring.

9 March 2026 Weeks 3–4

Beyond the Website: Expanding My Role

What I Worked On

The work these weeks stretched well beyond the SMCC office. A major new task was building the website and social media presence for Biznis Arena , an event co-organized by the Chamber aimed at Macedonian businesses looking to expand into Sweden. But what made this period especially interesting was getting to work more closely alongside Phoenix Rising, a union for young entrepreneurs that has a strong connection with SMCC. Some of the work crossed over into their space, different location, different people, different energy. The Chamber operates with a more institutional, cross-border feel, while Phoenix Rising runs on more of a startup-community vibe, driven by younger people who are actively building things. Both environments taught me something different about how professional networks function and how organizations with aligned missions can support each other without merging into one. A key realization across all of it was that digital work in a setting like this isn’t just about building things, it’s about connecting people and communicating value.

Deadlines & Planning

All deadlines for social media content and outreach tasks were met this period. The Biznis Arena website is progressing well and the current progress can be viewed at biznisarena.vercel.app. The next phase will also involve diving deeper into backend development. I’ve started researching technologies and architectures that could support the organization’s needs long-term, and there have been early conversations about whether a mobile app could add value. My plan for the next two weeks is to continue refining the Biznis Arena site, set up a development environment for backend work, and keep delivering consistent social media content.

What I Learned

I learned that content creation is a skill in itself. Writing a social media post that is both professional and engaging takes more thought than I initially assumed. I also learned about outreach strategies and how organizations build professional relationships, something I hadn’t been exposed to before. On the technical side, I started exploring backend frameworks and database options, which are new territories for me. I’m learning by reading documentation, watching tutorials, and discussing possibilities with colleagues who have more experience in this area.

The Environment

Getting to work across two different spaces, the Chamber and the Phoenix Rising community, gave me a more layered sense of what professional environments can look like. Phoenix Rising has a noticeably different energy: more informal, more builder-focused, and populated by people who are in the early stages of their entrepreneurial journey. The contrast with the Chamber’s more structured, diplomatically oriented atmosphere was striking. I found myself adapting quickly to each, more measured in one setting, more exploratory in the other. Both were welcoming, and both pushed me to think about what kind of professional environment I work best in. The international nature of the team continues to be one of the most enriching aspects of all of this, and sitting in on strategic meetings still feels like a rare opportunity for a student.

Strengths & Growth

I’m becoming more comfortable with multitasking across different types of work, switching between development, content creation, and communication tasks within the same day. I think my technical foundation is strong, and I’m building on it steadily. Where I still want to grow is in backend development, which is new for me, and in time management when juggling multiple responsibilities. I also want to improve my ability to take initiative in meetings and share ideas more confidently.

Looking Ahead

These weeks have reinforced how much I enjoy this internship. It’s fun to work on things that matter, with people who care about what they do. I’m gaining exposure to technologies and professional situations that I wouldn’t encounter in a classroom. The experience truly feels like it’s opening many doors, and I’m motivated to keep pushing myself. There’s real potential here, and I want to make the most of every remaining week.

23 March 2026 Weeks 5–6

Working on Blockchain Skopje: Design & Branding

What I Worked On

Alongside my internship, I’ve been co-founding Blockchain Skopje, a hackathon and summit taking place in Skopje aimed at bringing together builders, founders, and blockchain enthusiasts from the region. My role sits somewhere between product and design: I’m responsible for the visual identity of the event, everything from the overall branding direction to the individual assets we push out on social media. This meant starting from scratch and making a lot of early decisions that would set the tone for everything that came after. Defining the color palette, the gradient style, the typography, all of it had to feel cohesive and intentional. I drew inspiration from the raw, workshop-like energy of Base42, which led me toward a “builder” aesthetic: darker tones, bold gradients, a vibe that feels like something real is being made, not just marketed.

Deadlines & Planning

The event has a fixed date, which means the design work runs on a tight, content-driven schedule. Every week there are new assets to ship, speaker announcement cards, sponsor posts, ticket urgency graphics, and event reminders. Each one has to go out at the right moment and look consistent with everything that came before. I’ve had to think beyond just making things look good and start thinking about designing for conversion: clear calls to action, posts that communicate urgency (“80% of tickets sold”), and layouts that make it obvious what someone is supposed to do next. It’s a different mindset from just designing something visually pleasing, and it took a few iterations to get there.

What I Learned

The biggest design lesson so far has been learning to spot when something looks “AI-generated” and fixing it. Early drafts of some assets had that overly polished, slightly sterile quality that you see everywhere now. I started paying more attention to small things, irregular spacing, more deliberate font choices, avoiding the same layout patterns, to get the visuals to feel more human. I also learned a lot about designing across platforms. What works on Instagram doesn’t always translate to LinkedIn, and Discord has its own context entirely. Each platform needed slightly different framing, even when the content was the same. That kind of thinking, adapting the message to the medium, is something I hadn’t consciously practiced before.

The Environment

Working as a co-founder is a different experience from being an intern. There’s no one to hand off decisions to, if a design doesn’t work, that’s on me to catch and fix before it goes live. The feedback loop is fast, sometimes within hours of posting something I’m already thinking about what to change next time. It’s also been a good exercise in communicating design decisions to people who aren’t designers. When a co-founder asks “why does this look better?”, I have to actually articulate it rather than just going with gut feeling. That pressure to explain your thinking has made me more deliberate.

Strengths & Growth

I feel like I’ve gotten noticeably faster at producing design assets without sacrificing quality, which is something that only comes from doing it repeatedly under real pressure. Branding consistency has been one of my stronger points, keeping everything visually tied together across weeks of output takes discipline. Where I’ve had to grow is in the conversion-focused side of design. Thinking about what a post is trying to make someone do, not just feel, is a skill I’m still developing. I’m also getting better at knowing when to stop iterating and just ship something.

Looking Ahead

As the event gets closer, the pace of design work is picking up. There’s a lot still to produce, website updates, final event materials, and last-push promotional content. I’m excited to see how the full identity holds together in the weeks ahead, and more than anything, I’m curious to see how people respond to the event itself. It’s one thing to design for something in the abstract; it’s another to watch real people engage with it. That feedback will be the most useful thing I take away from this.

6 April 2026 Weeks 7–8

Ambassadors, Diplomacy, and a Different Kind of Networking

What I Worked On

These two weeks introduced me to a side of the internship I hadn’t anticipated: diplomatic-level networking. As part of my role at the Chamber, I had the opportunity to sit in on meetings with ambassadors from different countries, an experience that felt genuinely out of the ordinary for a student. The most significant were meetings connected to the Swedish Embassy in Macedonia and the Norwegian Embassy in Belgrade. The Swedish ambassador’s relationship with SMCC is close and longstanding, the Chamber’s entire reason for existing is to facilitate trade and relationships between Sweden and Macedonia, so that connection is central to what we do. The Norwegian side came through a different angle, but what struck me was how naturally the two embassies relate to each other. Sweden and Norway operate with a remarkably similar diplomatic philosophy, and you genuinely feel that in the room.

Deadlines & Planning

The ambassador meetings weren’t my primary deliverables for the week, but they took up significant mental space. Alongside them, I continued pushing forward on the usual development and content work. The priority for the coming weeks is to keep the SMCC digital presence updated and to channel what I learned from these high-level conversations into how we communicate the organization’s mission online. Understanding who the Chamber works with at this level makes it easier to make the right decisions about tone and positioning.

What I Learned

I learned that diplomacy and business development aren’t as separate as they sound. Both embassies were deeply invested in practical outcomes, trade, partnerships, visibility for their respective countries. The ambassadors weren’t symbolic figures; they were people actively thinking about how to create real connections. That realization changed how I think about the Chamber’s work. It’s not just a trade organization , it operates at the intersection of culture, politics, and commerce. Understanding that helps me make better decisions about how the digital presence should communicate that identity.

The Environment

Sitting in a room with an ambassador is a different kind of professional experience. The conversations are measured, the stakes feel higher, and the context is richer than a typical meeting. What surprised me most was how approachable both the Swedish and Norwegian ambassadors were. There was a genuine warmth in both interactions and a clear sense of shared purpose, Sweden and Norway have a strong bilateral relationship, and that comes through in how their diplomatic representatives engage with the same organizations. It made me appreciate how much of international relations runs on personal rapport as much as formal structure. The strong connection between the two embassies wasn’t something I had expected, but it was one of the more interesting things I took away from the whole experience.

Strengths & Growth

I felt more comfortable in these settings than I expected. I was quiet where it was appropriate to be quiet and engaged when given the opening. What I want to keep developing is my ability to ask better questions in high-context conversations, questions that add something rather than just confirming what’s already been said. That’s a skill that takes time, but I’m more aware of it now than I was before these weeks.

Looking Ahead

These weeks reinforced that this internship is exposing me to things I wouldn’t access through any other channel. Being in the room for conversations at that level, and understanding even a fraction of the context behind them, is rare. I want to make sure I’m drawing the right lessons from it, not just treating it as an interesting experience, but letting it genuinely shape how I think about international work and professional relationships going forward.

20 April 2026 Weeks 9–10

Rebranding the United Macedonian Diaspora

What I Worked On

These two weeks were dominated by one of the most substantial projects I’ve taken on during the internship: a full rebrand and website rebuild for the United Macedonian Diaspora, a global organization connecting Macedonians living around the world. The scope was broader than any previous project, it wasn’t just a new website, it was a rethink of the entire visual identity. That meant establishing a new direction for the brand, translating it into a coherent design system, building the site from the ground up, and then extending that identity across social media content. Each of those pieces fed into the others, which made it feel less like a series of tasks and more like a single, interconnected project that had to be held together consistently throughout.

Deadlines & Planning

The rebrand had a tight timeline given how much ground it covered. I worked through it by prioritizing the visual identity first, once the brand direction was clear, the website and social content followed more naturally. The main challenge was making sure decisions made early on held up across all the different surfaces the brand needed to appear on. A color choice that works on a website doesn’t automatically work on a social media post, and those gaps have to be caught before they compound. All deliverables were met within the timeframe, and the work moving into the next weeks is focused on refinements and ongoing content.

What I Learned

Rebranding an existing organization is meaningfully different from building something new. There’s an existing identity, even if it’s outdated, and an audience that has some relationship with it. Getting the balance right between modernizing and maintaining a sense of continuity required more careful thinking than I expected. I also learned a lot about what it takes to design for a diaspora organization specifically: the audience is geographically spread, culturally diverse within a shared identity, and the brand needs to feel inclusive across all of that. That informed a lot of the decisions I made around tone, imagery direction, and visual language.

The Environment

Working on the UMD project added another dimension to the internship. It connected to the broader mission of the Chamber in an interesting way, both organizations are, at their core, about building and maintaining Macedonian connections across borders. Working across multiple organizations with overlapping but distinct missions has been one of the more unusual aspects of this internship, and it’s given me a much wider view of the ecosystem than I would have gotten working within a single company.

Strengths & Growth

The scope of this project pushed my ability to hold a consistent creative direction across a long stretch of work. I think that’s something I’ve genuinely improved at. Where I want to grow is in the handoff side of things, making sure the brand guidelines I establish are documented clearly enough that someone else could pick them up and continue without losing the thread. That discipline is something I’m still building.

Looking Ahead

Completing a full rebrand for a real international organization is the kind of work I didn’t expect to be doing at this stage. The fact that it’s live and being used is a good reminder that the internship has produced tangible outcomes, not just learning experiences. That’s something I want to carry into the final weeks, keeping the quality high and finishing strong.

4 May 2026 Weeks 11–12

Building Mastorify: Apps, Startups, and the AI Boom

What I Worked On

These weeks marked a significant shift in the direction my supervisor has been pushing me toward. Beyond the organizational work I’d been doing at the Chamber, he started actively encouraging me to move into building new apps and designing the product experiences around them. The context behind that push is hard to ignore: we’re in the middle of an AI boom, and the window for building smart, well-timed products has never felt more open. The project that came out of that conversation is Mastorify, a mobile app targeting the Greek market. The core idea is straightforward but genuinely useful: help customers find the right service providers, and help service providers find more customers. Think of it as a marketplace built around trust and discoverability, targeting an underserved gap in how local services are discovered and booked in Greece. These weeks were spent on early product design, mapping user flows, and building out the initial app structure.

Deadlines & Planning

Mastorify doesn’t have a fixed launch deadline yet, but the work is moving in parallel to my remaining internship weeks. The immediate priorities were getting the core concept clearly defined, validating that the problem is real, and establishing the design direction for the app. Alongside the product work, I also started researching what it would actually take to run a business in Greece as someone who doesn’t reside there, tax structures, company registration options, whether a sole trader setup or a registered entity makes more sense at this stage. That research is ongoing and is shaping how I think about the timeline for a proper launch.

What I Learned

The legal and administrative side of starting a business in a foreign country is genuinely complex. Greece has its own requirements around business registration, VAT, and what operating as a non-resident entails, and a lot of those details aren’t easy to find in English. I also spent time thinking through different business models: subscription vs. commission-based, whether to charge service providers or customers or both, how to build trust early when the platform has no track record. These aren’t theoretical exercises, they directly affect what gets built and in what order. I’ve come to appreciate that the business plan and the product are inseparable at this stage. What you decide to charge shapes who your users are and how the product has to behave.

The Environment

Having a supervisor who actively pushes you to think bigger is something I didn’t expect going into this internship. The conversation that led to Mastorify wasn’t a task assignment, it was more of a challenge: given everything you’ve built here, what could you build for yourself? That framing changed how I approached the work. I wasn’t executing someone else’s brief anymore, I was making the decisions, owning the direction, and living with the consequences of those choices. That’s a different kind of pressure, and a more motivating one.

Strengths & Growth

I’ve found that I can move quickly from concept to working prototype when the idea is clear enough in my head. The design work for Mastorify came together faster than I expected because I had a strong mental model of the problem from the start. Where I’m growing is in startup thinking more broadly, understanding how to validate before building too much, how to structure a business model that actually makes money, and how to think about legal and operational infrastructure without letting it slow the momentum down completely. These aren’t skills that come from courses. They come from doing.

Looking Ahead

Mastorify is the most personally exciting thing to come out of this internship. It sits at the intersection of everything I’ve been building toward: product thinking, design, development, and now the business layer on top of all of that. The AI boom isn’t slowing down, and if there’s a moment to build something real, this feels like it. I’m committed to seeing it through past the internship, and the foundation laid in these weeks gives me genuine confidence that it can become something.

15 May 2026 Weeks 13–14

The Bigger Picture: Networks, Outsourcing, and Our First Real Clients

What I Worked On

As the internship reaches its final weeks, something that has been quietly taking shape throughout the whole period is now becoming clear: everything I’ve been working on has been part of building an outsourcing business. Not a hypothetical one, a real one, with a deliberate strategy behind it. The approach was straightforward but smart: start by finding real clients, do most of the work at a reduced or no cost, gain genuine experience, build a real portfolio, and establish trust with organizations that would otherwise never take a chance on someone starting out. Every website, every rebrand, every social media campaign, SMCC, Phoenix Rising, United Macedonian Diaspora, Blockchain Skopje, Biznis Arena, all of it was laying the groundwork. These are not just projects. They will be our first clients, the ones I can proudly put on the website of the business we are building together.

Deadlines & Planning

The immediate focus is on documenting the work clearly enough that it can be presented professionally on the outsourcing business website. That means writing proper case studies, collecting results where we have them, and framing each project in terms of the business problem it solved, not just the technical or creative work involved. The business itself is still being structured, but the portfolio already exists. That’s an unusual position to be in, and it’s one of the things that makes this internship genuinely different from a typical placement.

What I Learned

The biggest lesson of these final weeks is about how to build something from zero without any of the usual shortcuts. You can’t pitch a portfolio you don’t have, and you can’t charge what you’re worth before you’ve proven what you’re worth. The strategy of investing time and effort upfront, doing serious work for organizations that gave us a chance, is the only honest path to having something real to show. I’ve also learned that building a business alongside an internship requires treating every piece of work as both a deliverable and an asset. The discipline of thinking that way changes how you approach even the smallest task.

The Environment

One of the things that has genuinely surprised me about this internship is how much my network has grown. I came in as a student and I’m leaving with connections across IT, business, diplomacy, entrepreneurship, and the startup world, people I would never have met through university alone. Ambassadors, founders, business consultants, developers, event organizers, diaspora leaders. Each project brought a different set of people into my orbit, and many of those relationships have turned into ongoing conversations. That network is, in many ways, the most valuable thing I’m taking out of this experience. A portfolio can be rebuilt; the right connections, built on real shared work, are much harder to manufacture.

Strengths & Growth

I’ve become more comfortable thinking in terms of business outcomes rather than just deliverables. That shift, from “what did I build?” to “what did this achieve, and for whom?”, is something I want to keep. Where I still want to grow is in the commercial side: pricing work properly, having direct conversations about scope and value, and not underselling what I can actually deliver. The confidence to charge appropriately comes from having the portfolio to back it up. I’m getting there.

Looking Ahead

This internship ends on paper in May, but what it set in motion doesn’t. The outsourcing business continues. Mastorify continues. The network continues to grow. I came in as a student looking for experience and I’m leaving as someone with real clients, a real portfolio, and a real business being built. That wasn’t the plan when I started, but looking back, it was exactly the right outcome.

Key Skills Developed

What I actually built, learned, and can now apply

💡

Product Thinking

From ideas to working things

Most of the work here involved making decisions that had real consequences. Building the SMCC website wasn’t just a development task, it required understanding what the organization wanted to communicate, who their audience was, and how to translate that into a structure that made sense to a first-time visitor. The same applied to Biznis Arena and Blockchain Skopje. In each case I had to think about what the product was trying to achieve before I could make any useful decisions about how it should look or work. That shift, from “what does this look like?” to “what is this trying to do?”, is something I didn’t fully appreciate before this internship.

🎨

Design & Branding

Visual identity and consistency

The Blockchain Skopje project pushed me to think seriously about visual identity in a way that school projects never quite did. I had to establish a visual language from scratch, colors, gradients, typography, and then apply it consistently across weeks of social media output, speaker cards, sponsor posts, and event graphics. The recurring challenge was making things feel considered rather than generated. I started paying attention to font weight contrast, spacing decisions, and layout variety, because those small choices are what separate something that looks real from something that looks templated.

💻

Web Development

Building and shipping

I built and launched three websites during this internship: the SMCC platform, Biznis Arena, and the Blockchain Skopje site. The SMCC work went beyond front-end, I implemented a backend using Supabase, handling the database, authentication, and API layer. That was my first time working with a real backend on a live product, and it changed how I think about front-end code too. The Biznis Arena and Blockchain Skopje sites had tighter timelines and clearer conversion goals, which pushed me to make faster, more confident decisions.

📣

Communication & Outreach

Writing for real audiences

Writing social media content for a professional organization is harder than it sounds. Every post has to be accurate, appropriately toned, and short enough to actually be read. I learned to adapt the same core message for different platforms, what works on LinkedIn doesn’t work on Instagram, and Discord is its own context entirely. I also got involved in outreach efforts, which gave me a direct look at how organizations build professional relationships. Being clear and proactive in communication, especially in a cross-cultural, two-country setup, became something I consciously worked on throughout.

🗓️

Event & Operations Thinking

Understanding how the pieces fit together

Working on Blockchain Skopje gave me a view of what it actually takes to organize an event from the ground up. The design work fed directly into operations: urgency posts had real ticket quotas behind them, speaker cards had to go out on specific dates, sponsor content had to be approved before it could be published. I started to understand how different workstreams of an event fit together and how each design asset was serving a broader operational goal. It changed how I think about design, it’s not just visual output, it’s part of how something runs.

Challenges & Problem Solving

What didn’t go smoothly, and how I navigated it

01

Payment Infrastructure Constraints

Stripe and similar payment processors aren’t straightforwardly available for events operating in this region without a registered company and proper business setup. For Blockchain Skopje, this meant finding workarounds for ticket sales that didn’t rely on standard checkout infrastructure. It required researching alternatives quickly and accepting that the experience wouldn’t be as seamless as it would be with full payment tool support. The lesson: real constraints force creative solutions, and sometimes the “obvious” tool simply isn’t available.

02

Operating Without a Registered Entity

Not being a registered company affects more than just payments, it touches how sponsors engage with you, how agreements are structured, and how much trust you can build quickly with external parties. A lot of the infrastructure around running events assumes you already have a legal entity in place. Working around that required more manual coordination and transparency in every conversation. It was a useful reminder that the operational side of a project is just as important as the product side.

03

Managing Multiple Platforms Simultaneously

Instagram, LinkedIn, Discord, a website, and email were all live at the same time, each with its own content cadence, tone, and audience. Keeping everything consistent without letting anything slip required a level of organization I had to build quickly. The challenge wasn’t just having enough content; it was making sure the right content went to the right place at the right time, and that nothing contradicted anything else. It made me appreciate how much invisible coordination goes into running a coherent online presence.

04

Tight Timelines Under Event Pressure

Design assets for Blockchain Skopje often had to go out within a day of being conceived, especially for time-sensitive announcements. That pace leaves little room for extended iteration. I had to get better at producing something good enough quickly, rather than waiting until it felt perfect. That’s a harder shift than it sounds when you care about the quality of what you’re putting out. Learning to draw that line, good enough versus needs more work, was one of the more practical skills I developed.

05

Communicating Across Technical and Non-Technical Boundaries

Much of the feedback I received came from people who weren’t thinking in terms of code or design systems. Translating between what a stakeholder wanted and what was technically or practically feasible, without making it feel like a negotiation, was something I had to figure out as I went. It pushed me to explain decisions more clearly and to understand that what I build only matters if the people around me understand what it does and why it was built that way.

Tools & Technologies

What I used and how I actually used it

⌨️  Development

HTML / CSS / JavaScript

Core stack for both the SMCC website and Biznis Arena. No framework, everything written by hand, which kept things simple and fast to iterate on.

Vite

Local dev server with hot reload. Made the development loop noticeably faster compared to working directly with a file browser.

Vercel

Used to deploy and host both sites. Fast deploys from Git, with a shareable preview URL useful for getting feedback before going live.

Supabase

Backend-as-a-service used for the SMCC platform, database, authentication, and API layer. Handled real data without needing to build a full backend from scratch.

Git

Version control for both projects. Helped keep track of changes and made it easier to roll back when something broke.

🎨  Design

Figma

Used for planning layouts and exploring visual identity before moving to code. Especially useful for Blockchain Skopje, where the visual direction needed to be established early.

Canva

Main tool for producing social media assets at speed, speaker cards, sponsor posts, urgency graphics. Fast enough to keep up with the event content calendar.

📋  Operations

Google Workspace

Shared documents, email, and file management across the SMCC team. The main tool for anything that needed to be written, stored, or sent formally.

Notion

Used for personal planning, content tracking, and keeping notes across multiple workstreams. Helped maintain clarity when switching between projects.

💬  Communication

Discord

Primary coordination channel for Blockchain Skopje, community announcements, team discussions, and day-to-day event planning.

Instagram & LinkedIn

Social media presence for both SMCC and Blockchain Skopje. Each platform served a different audience and required a different tone and format.

Email

Formal outreach and stakeholder communication. Still the default for anything that needed a paper trail or a more considered response.

Final Reflection

Looking back on what this internship actually was

The most obvious difference between this internship and any school project is that the work ships. It goes live, it gets visited by real people, and if something is wrong, someone notices. That changes how you approach every decision. I started this internship confident in my front-end skills and less sure about everything else. By the end, I realized that the technical part was often the easier part, what’s harder is knowing what to build, understanding why it matters, and communicating that to everyone involved.

One of the harder adjustments was learning to let go of things. In school, there’s usually time to iterate until something feels right. Here, there were deadlines that didn’t move, events that happened regardless, and stakeholders who needed things done. I learned to distinguish between what actually needed to be good and what just needed to be finished, and to ship things that weren’t quite where I wanted them, as long as they were functional and honest. That’s a harder line to draw than it sounds.

What I didn’t expect was how much my network would grow. I came in as a student and I’m leaving with connections across IT, business, diplomacy, startups, and the Macedonian diaspora worldwide, people I would never have met through university alone. Each project pulled a different set of people into my orbit, and many of those relationships have outlasted the individual work. That network is, in many ways, the most valuable thing I’m taking out of this experience.

The clearest thing I understand now, looking back, is that every project was part of something larger: building the portfolio and the client base for an outsourcing business. The strategy was deliberate, find real organizations, do serious work, earn trust, and it worked. SMCC, Phoenix Rising, United Macedonian Diaspora, Blockchain Skopje, Biznis Arena: these aren’t just items on a CV. They are our first clients. And the business we are building will launch with them already on its website.

I came in thinking of myself primarily as a developer. I’m leaving as someone who builds products, understands businesses, navigates international networks, and is actively co-building a company. The internship ends on paper. What it set in motion doesn’t.
