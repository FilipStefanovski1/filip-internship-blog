23 February 2026 Weeks 1–2

First Impressions and Building the Foundation

What I Worked On

The first two weeks at the Swedish Macedonian Chamber of Commerce have been a whirlwind. My primary task was to build the organization’s website from scratch. I was given a brief and some brand guidelines, and from there it was largely up to me to plan the structure, design the layout, and implement everything. This required strong front-end skills like HTML, CSS, and JavaScript, as well as an understanding of user experience principles from my studies. One of my biggest realizations was how different it is to build something for a real organization compared to a school project. Every decision carries more weight when real users are going to interact with the result.

Deadlines & Planning

The initial deadline for the website’s first version was met on time. I planned my work by breaking the site into sections and tackling them one by one. I have now been working on the website for over a month and a half, and I am already contributing to the team’s social media output as well. For the coming weeks, the plan is to continue refining the website based on feedback and begin exploring the backend requirements.

What I Learned

Beyond the technical skills I practiced, I learned a great deal about working within an organization that operates across two countries. Communication happens in different time zones and across cultural boundaries, which taught me the importance of being clear and proactive in my messages. I also gained a deeper appreciation for how much effort goes into managing an organization from the inside. There are so many moving parts that aren’t visible from the outside.

The Environment

The team is larger than I expected, over 20 people, and everyone has been welcoming. I’ve already met several professionals from various business backgrounds, which has been eye-opening. My colleagues come with different values and working styles, and I’m learning to adapt my communication accordingly. The work environment is quite pleasant and motivating, which makes it easy to stay engaged. Being treated as “the IT guy” from day one was a bit surprising, but it also gave me an immediate sense of responsibility and ownership over the digital side of things.

Strengths & Growth

I feel confident in my front-end development skills, which allowed me to deliver the website on schedule. Where I see room for growth is in project estimation. I underestimated how long certain design decisions would take when they need stakeholder approval. On the soft-skills side, I’m getting better at professional communication, but I want to improve my ability to present technical ideas to non-technical colleagues.

Looking Ahead

Overall, I’m genuinely enjoying this internship. The work is engaging, the people are interesting, and I can already feel that this experience is opening doors. I see real potential in creating a digital solution here that could become something valuable, not just for the organization, but as a learning experience for my future career. I’m excited for what the coming weeks will bring.

9 March 2026 Weeks 3–4

Beyond the Website: Expanding My Role

What I Worked On

With the organization’s main website launched and live, these two weeks marked a shift in my responsibilities. A major new task has been creating a website and building the social media presence for an upcoming event called Biznis Arena. This has been a great opportunity to apply my development skills to a real project with a clear deadline and audience. Alongside that, I continued creating social media posts for the organization, which required a different kind of thinking: concise messaging, visual consistency, and understanding what resonates with a professional audience. I also started supporting outreach efforts and got my first exposure to the outsourcing branch of the organization, which gave me insight into how business development works in practice. A key realization was that digital work in an organization like this isn’t just about building things. It’s about connecting people and communicating value.

Deadlines & Planning

All deadlines for social media content and outreach tasks were met this period. The Biznis Arena website is progressing well and the current progress can be viewed at biznisarena.vercel.app. The next phase will also involve diving deeper into backend development. I’ve started researching technologies and architectures that could support the organization’s needs long-term, and there have been early conversations about whether a mobile app could add value. My plan for the next two weeks is to continue refining the Biznis Arena site, set up a development environment for backend work, and keep delivering consistent social media content.

What I Learned

I learned that content creation is a skill in itself. Writing a social media post that is both professional and engaging takes more thought than I initially assumed. I also learned about outreach strategies and how organizations build professional relationships, something I hadn’t been exposed to before. On the technical side, I started exploring backend frameworks and database options, which are new territories for me. I’m learning by reading documentation, watching tutorials, and discussing possibilities with colleagues who have more experience in this area.

The Environment

The international nature of the team continues to be one of the most enriching aspects of this internship. Working with people from different professional backgrounds, from business consultants to marketing specialists, has broadened my perspective significantly. I’ve had the opportunity to sit in on meetings where businesspeople discuss strategy and partnerships, which feels like a rare opportunity for a student. The atmosphere is professional but also supportive, and the overall work environment continues to be pleasant and motivating. I appreciate that my contributions are taken seriously despite being an intern.

Strengths & Growth

I’m becoming more comfortable with multitasking across different types of work, switching between development, content creation, and communication tasks within the same day. I think my technical foundation is strong, and I’m building on it steadily. Where I still want to grow is in backend development, which is new for me, and in time management when juggling multiple responsibilities. I also want to improve my ability to take initiative in meetings and share ideas more confidently.

Looking Ahead

These weeks have reinforced how much I enjoy this internship. It’s fun to work on things that matter, with people who care about what they do. I’m gaining exposure to technologies and professional situations that I wouldn’t encounter in a classroom. The experience truly feels like it’s opening many doors, and I’m motivated to keep pushing myself. There’s real potential here, and I want to make the most of every remaining week.

23 March 2026 Weeks 5–6

Working on Blockchain Skopje: Design & Branding

What I Worked On

Alongside my internship, I’ve been co-founding Blockchain Skopje — a hackathon and summit taking place in Skopje aimed at bringing together builders, founders, and blockchain enthusiasts from the region. My role sits somewhere between product and design: I’m responsible for the visual identity of the event, everything from the overall branding direction to the individual assets we push out on social media. This meant starting from scratch and making a lot of early decisions that would set the tone for everything that came after. Defining the color palette, the gradient style, the typography — all of it had to feel cohesive and intentional. I drew inspiration from the raw, workshop-like energy of Base42, which led me toward a “builder” aesthetic: darker tones, bold gradients, a vibe that feels like something real is being made, not just marketed.

Deadlines & Planning

The event has a fixed date, which means the design work runs on a tight, content-driven schedule. Every week there are new assets to ship — speaker announcement cards, sponsor posts, ticket urgency graphics, and event reminders. Each one has to go out at the right moment and look consistent with everything that came before. I’ve had to think beyond just making things look good and start thinking about designing for conversion: clear calls to action, posts that communicate urgency (“80% of tickets sold”), and layouts that make it obvious what someone is supposed to do next. It’s a different mindset from just designing something visually pleasing, and it took a few iterations to get there.

What I Learned

The biggest design lesson so far has been learning to spot when something looks “AI-generated” and fixing it. Early drafts of some assets had that overly polished, slightly sterile quality that you see everywhere now. I started paying more attention to small things — irregular spacing, more deliberate font choices, avoiding the same layout patterns — to get the visuals to feel more human. I also learned a lot about designing across platforms. What works on Instagram doesn’t always translate to LinkedIn, and Discord has its own context entirely. Each platform needed slightly different framing, even when the content was the same. That kind of thinking — adapting the message to the medium — is something I hadn’t consciously practiced before.

The Environment

Working as a co-founder is a different experience from being an intern. There’s no one to hand off decisions to — if a design doesn’t work, that’s on me to catch and fix before it goes live. The feedback loop is fast, sometimes within hours of posting something I’m already thinking about what to change next time. It’s also been a good exercise in communicating design decisions to people who aren’t designers. When a co-founder asks “why does this look better?”, I have to actually articulate it rather than just going with gut feeling. That pressure to explain your thinking has made me more deliberate.

Strengths & Growth

I feel like I’ve gotten noticeably faster at producing design assets without sacrificing quality, which is something that only comes from doing it repeatedly under real pressure. Branding consistency has been one of my stronger points — keeping everything visually tied together across weeks of output takes discipline. Where I’ve had to grow is in the conversion-focused side of design. Thinking about what a post is trying to make someone do, not just feel, is a skill I’m still developing. I’m also getting better at knowing when to stop iterating and just ship something.

Looking Ahead

As the event gets closer, the pace of design work is picking up. There’s a lot still to produce — website updates, final event materials, and last-push promotional content. I’m excited to see how the full identity holds together in the weeks ahead, and more than anything, I’m curious to see how people respond to the event itself. It’s one thing to design for something in the abstract; it’s another to watch real people engage with it. That feedback will be the most useful thing I take away from this.

Key Skills Developed

What I actually built, learned, and can now apply

💡

Product Thinking

From ideas to working things

Most of the work here involved making decisions that had real consequences. Building the SMCC website wasn’t just a development task — it required understanding what the organization wanted to communicate, who their audience was, and how to translate that into a structure that made sense to a first-time visitor. The same applied to Biznis Arena and Blockchain Skopje. In each case I had to think about what the product was trying to achieve before I could make any useful decisions about how it should look or work. That shift — from “what does this look like?” to “what is this trying to do?” — is something I didn’t fully appreciate before this internship.

🎨

Design & Branding

Visual identity and consistency

The Blockchain Skopje project pushed me to think seriously about visual identity in a way that school projects never quite did. I had to establish a visual language from scratch — colors, gradients, typography — and then apply it consistently across weeks of social media output, speaker cards, sponsor posts, and event graphics. The recurring challenge was making things feel considered rather than generated. I started paying attention to font weight contrast, spacing decisions, and layout variety, because those small choices are what separate something that looks real from something that looks templated.

💻

Web Development

Building and shipping

I built and launched three websites during this internship: the SMCC platform, Biznis Arena, and the Blockchain Skopje site. The SMCC work went beyond front-end — I implemented a backend using Supabase, handling the database, authentication, and API layer. That was my first time working with a real backend on a live product, and it changed how I think about front-end code too. The Biznis Arena and Blockchain Skopje sites had tighter timelines and clearer conversion goals, which pushed me to make faster, more confident decisions.

📣

Communication & Outreach

Writing for real audiences

Writing social media content for a professional organization is harder than it sounds. Every post has to be accurate, appropriately toned, and short enough to actually be read. I learned to adapt the same core message for different platforms — what works on LinkedIn doesn’t work on Instagram, and Discord is its own context entirely. I also got involved in outreach efforts, which gave me a direct look at how organizations build professional relationships. Being clear and proactive in communication — especially in a cross-cultural, two-country setup — became something I consciously worked on throughout.

🗓️

Event & Operations Thinking

Understanding how the pieces fit together

Working on Blockchain Skopje gave me a view of what it actually takes to organize an event from the ground up. The design work fed directly into operations: urgency posts had real ticket quotas behind them, speaker cards had to go out on specific dates, sponsor content had to be approved before it could be published. I started to understand how different workstreams of an event fit together and how each design asset was serving a broader operational goal. It changed how I think about design — it’s not just visual output, it’s part of how something runs.

Challenges & Problem Solving

What didn’t go smoothly, and how I navigated it

01

Payment Infrastructure Constraints

Stripe and similar payment processors aren’t straightforwardly available for events operating in this region without a registered company and proper business setup. For Blockchain Skopje, this meant finding workarounds for ticket sales that didn’t rely on standard checkout infrastructure. It required researching alternatives quickly and accepting that the experience wouldn’t be as seamless as it would be with full payment tool support. The lesson: real constraints force creative solutions, and sometimes the “obvious” tool simply isn’t available.

02

Operating Without a Registered Entity

Not being a registered company affects more than just payments — it touches how sponsors engage with you, how agreements are structured, and how much trust you can build quickly with external parties. A lot of the infrastructure around running events assumes you already have a legal entity in place. Working around that required more manual coordination and transparency in every conversation. It was a useful reminder that the operational side of a project is just as important as the product side.

03

Managing Multiple Platforms Simultaneously

Instagram, LinkedIn, Discord, a website, and email were all live at the same time — each with its own content cadence, tone, and audience. Keeping everything consistent without letting anything slip required a level of organization I had to build quickly. The challenge wasn’t just having enough content; it was making sure the right content went to the right place at the right time, and that nothing contradicted anything else. It made me appreciate how much invisible coordination goes into running a coherent online presence.

04

Tight Timelines Under Event Pressure

Design assets for Blockchain Skopje often had to go out within a day of being conceived, especially for time-sensitive announcements. That pace leaves little room for extended iteration. I had to get better at producing something good enough quickly, rather than waiting until it felt perfect. That’s a harder shift than it sounds when you care about the quality of what you’re putting out. Learning to draw that line — good enough versus needs more work — was one of the more practical skills I developed.

05

Communicating Across Technical and Non-Technical Boundaries

Much of the feedback I received came from people who weren’t thinking in terms of code or design systems. Translating between what a stakeholder wanted and what was technically or practically feasible — without making it feel like a negotiation — was something I had to figure out as I went. It pushed me to explain decisions more clearly and to understand that what I build only matters if the people around me understand what it does and why it was built that way.

Tools & Technologies

What I used and how I actually used it

⌨️  Development

HTML / CSS / JavaScript

Core stack for both the SMCC website and Biznis Arena. No framework — everything written by hand, which kept things simple and fast to iterate on.

Vite

Local dev server with hot reload. Made the development loop noticeably faster compared to working directly with a file browser.

Vercel

Used to deploy and host both sites. Fast deploys from Git, with a shareable preview URL useful for getting feedback before going live.

Supabase

Backend-as-a-service used for the SMCC platform — database, authentication, and API layer. Handled real data without needing to build a full backend from scratch.

Git

Version control for both projects. Helped keep track of changes and made it easier to roll back when something broke.

🎨  Design

Figma

Used for planning layouts and exploring visual identity before moving to code. Especially useful for Blockchain Skopje, where the visual direction needed to be established early.

Canva

Main tool for producing social media assets at speed — speaker cards, sponsor posts, urgency graphics. Fast enough to keep up with the event content calendar.

📋  Operations

Google Workspace

Shared documents, email, and file management across the SMCC team. The main tool for anything that needed to be written, stored, or sent formally.

Notion

Used for personal planning, content tracking, and keeping notes across multiple workstreams. Helped maintain clarity when switching between projects.

💬  Communication

Discord

Primary coordination channel for Blockchain Skopje — community announcements, team discussions, and day-to-day event planning.

Instagram & LinkedIn

Social media presence for both SMCC and Blockchain Skopje. Each platform served a different audience and required a different tone and format.

Email

Formal outreach and stakeholder communication. Still the default for anything that needed a paper trail or a more considered response.

Final Reflection

Looking back on what this internship actually was

The most obvious difference between this internship and any school project is that the work ships. It goes live, it gets visited by real people, and if something is wrong, someone notices. That changes how you approach every decision. I started this internship confident in my front-end skills and less sure about everything else. By the end, I realized that the technical part was often the easier part — what’s harder is knowing what to build, understanding why it matters, and communicating that to everyone involved.

One of the harder adjustments was learning to let go of things. In school, there’s usually time to iterate until something feels right. Here, there were deadlines that didn’t move, events that happened regardless, and stakeholders who needed things done. I learned to distinguish between what actually needed to be good and what just needed to be finished — and to ship things that weren’t quite where I wanted them, as long as they were functional and honest. That’s a harder line to draw than it sounds.

I came in thinking of myself primarily as a developer. What this internship showed me is that the lines between design, development, communication, and operations are blurrier than they look from the outside. On any given day I might be writing a social media post, pushing code, making a branding decision, or figuring out why something wasn’t working on mobile. That variety is what made the experience worthwhile — and it pushed me to think more holistically about what digital work actually involves.

I leave this internship with a clearer sense of what I’m capable of and where I still have room to grow. The combination of building real products, working inside a real organization, and co-founding a real event gave me a foundation I couldn’t have gotten in a classroom. I’m a more complete thinker now — not just about code or design, but about how things work and how to make them work better.
